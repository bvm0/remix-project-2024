An Overview of Ethics and the Minimum Wage

The United States' first federal minimum wage was signed into law by President Franklin D. Roosevelt in 1938.1 Since its inception, it had been raised regularly — once every few years, on average — until it reached $7.25 in 2009; up to this point, the longest period of time with no increase was ten years, from 1997 to 2007.2 However, since 2009, the federal minimum wage has seen no increases despite an ever-increasing cost of living.3 As a result, various movements to raise the minimum wage — both on the state and federal levels — have emerged, largely citing research conducted in the 1990s and early 2000s. However, one flaw with many of the arguments made for raising the minimum wage is that they tend to take a clearly consequentialist approach.

An examination of the arguments for and against raising the minimum wage from the perspectives of various normative philosophies is crucial to ensure policymakers are equipped to consider the views of all of their constituents, to enable the citizenry to understand the stances of their peers, and to enable Americans to reconcile their position on the minimum wage with the ethical principles they identify with. This website provides an overview of the arguments that seem mst natural from a variety of moral philosophies, consequentialist and other.
